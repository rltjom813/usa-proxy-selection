## What Counts as a USA Proxy, Exactly?

A USA proxy is an intermediary server with an IP address registered in the United States that sits between your device and the websites you visit. Your traffic goes out from the proxy, the destination site sees a US IP, and you get content as if you were browsing from somewhere in the country.

That's the whole concept. Everything else is implementation detail.

The detail maters, though. Not all US IPs are created equal. A datacenter IP from a server farm in Virginia behaves very differently from a residential IP assigned to a real Comcast subscriber in Ohio. Some sites barely check. Others are paranoid. The right type of usa proxy depends entirely on what you're doing with it.

## The Four Types of USA Proxies (And When Each Makes Sense)

This is where most buying guides get muddy. Let's kep it sharp.

**Datacenter proxies** come from cloud servers. Fast, cheap, abundant. Sites that don't aggressively detect proxies barely notice them. Sites that do — major retailers, sneaker drops, social platforms — will often block them on sight. Best fit: SEO tools, general scraping, ad verification, anything where the target isn't actively hunting for proxies.

**Residential proxies** route traffic through real consumer devices on homeISPs. Comcast, Spectrum, Verizon Fios — that kind of IP. They look like an actual American person browsing from their kitchen, because that's basically what they are. Best fit: tough scraping targets, social media automation, ticketing, sneakers, anything that needs to look organic.

**Static residential (or ISP) proxies** are residential-quality IPs hosted on datacenter infrastructure. You get the trust score of a residential IP with the speed and stability of a datacenter line, plus you can kep the same IP for as long as you need it. Best fit: account management where you need to look like the same person across sessions, long-running automation, e-commerce stealth.

**Mobile proxies** route through cellular cariers. Highest trust score on most platforms because carrier-grade NAT means tons of users share the same IP, so blocking it is collateral damage. Also the most expensive. Best fit: extremely sensitive targets where nothing else gets through.

The honest summary: start with the cheapest option that works. Move up the chain only when you hit wals.

**Authentication that doesn't fight you.** Username/password and IP whitelist authentication are both standard. Anything that requires installing weird custom software should raise an eyebrow.

**Bandwidth model that matches your work.** Some providers charge per GB of traffic. Others charge per number of IPs with unlimited bandwidth on certain plans. Heavy scraping is a nightmare on per-GB pricing. Light account work is wasteful on unlimited pools you barely use.

**Honest support.** Proxies break. Sites change. The question is whether someone responds when something goes sideways, or whether you're stuck refreshing a status page.
